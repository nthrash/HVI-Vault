### Diaphragm Ultrasound Assessment: A Clinical Reference Guide for Respiratory Failure and Post-Cardiac Intervention Patients

## Overview  
Diaphragm ultrasound is a noninvasive, reproducible bedside tool for assessing diaphragmatic function in critically ill patients with respiratory failure. It provides real-time assessment of diaphragm thickness, thickening fraction, and excursion, enabling clinicians to diagnose diaphragm dysfunction, predict weaning outcomes, and monitor recovery.[[
  
---  
## Measurement Technique
**Probe Placement:** High-frequency linear transducer (7.5-10 MHz) placed at the zone of apposition of the diaphragm to the rib cage, typically at the 8th-10th intercostal space along the mid-axillary line.
  
**Key Measurements:**  
1. **Diaphragm Thickness (Tdi):** Measured at end-expiration (functional residual capacity) and end-inspiration (total lung capacity) using B-mode imaging  
2. **Thickening Fraction (TFdi):** Calculated as: (Tdi at end-inspiration − Tdi at end-expiration) / Tdi at end-expiration × 100%  
3. **Diaphragmatic Excursion:** Measured using M-mode at the subcostal window, assessing craniocaudal displacement of the diaphragmatic dome  
  
**Technical Considerations:**  
- Right hemidiaphragm measurements are more reliable than left (repeatability coefficient 0.2 mm)
- Serial measurements by the same operator are preferable due to variable inter-observer repeatability (~18%)[[
- Visual assessment and diaphragm excursion show good inter-rater reliability; TFdi shows poor inter-rater reliability

## Normal Reference Values
**Diaphragm Thickness at End-Expiration (Adults):

| Parameter                        | Men        | Women      |
| -------------------------------- | ---------- | ---------- |
| Normal range                     | 1.5-2.5 mm | 1.5-2.5 mm |
| Lower limit of normal            | 1.3 mm     | 1.1 mm     |
| Abnormal side-to-side difference | >3.3 mm    | >3.3 mm    |
  
**Diaphragm Thickness at Total Lung Capacity:

| Parameter             | Men    | Women  |
| --------------------- | ------ | ------ |
| Lower limit of normal | 4.6 mm | 3.5 mm |
  
**Thickening Fraction (TFdi) During Deep Inspiration:**

| Parameter             | Men     | Women   |
| --------------------- | ------- | ------- |
| Normal range (right)  | 57-200% | 60-229% |
| Normal range (left)   | 60-260% | 58-264% |
| Lower limit of normal | 39-40%  | 39-48%  |

| Breathing Maneuver            | Men    | Women  |
| ----------------------------- | ------ | ------ |

| Maximum voluntary sniff (LLN) | 2.0 cm | 1.5 cm |
| Deep breathing to TLC (LLN)   | 7.9 cm | 6.4 cm |

## Diagnostic Criteria for Diaphragm Paralysis
**Unilateral Hemidiaphragm Paralysis:**
- TFdi 20% during deep inspiration on affected side  
- Reduced thickness on paralyzed side (1.7 ± 0.2 mm) vs. normal side (2.7 ± 0.5 mm)  
- Paradoxical (cranial) movement during voluntary sniffing in nearly all cases  
- Absent or paradoxical excursion during quiet breathing  
- Compensatory hyperactivity of contralateral hemidiaphragm (increased TFdi and excursion)  
  
**Bilateral Diaphragm Paralysis:
- TFdi 20% bilaterally (typically -1 ± 15%)  
- Reduced thickness bilaterally (1.8 ± 0.2 mm)  
- Absent or paradoxical excursion bilaterally  
- No compensatory mechanism available  
  
**Key Distinguishing Features:**  
  
| Parameter | Unilateral Paralysis | Bilateral Paralysis |  
|-----------|---------------------|---------------------|  
| Affected side TFdi | 20% (often negative) | 20% bilaterally |  
| Affected side thickness | 1.7 ± 0.2 mm | 1.8 ± 0.2 mm bilaterally |  
| Contralateral TFdi | Increased (compensatory) | 20% |  
| Contralateral excursion | Increased | Absent/paradoxical |  
| Respiratory reserve | Partially preserved | Severely compromised |

## Clinical Applications in Respiratory Failure
**Predicting Extubation Success:**
- Diaphragmatic excursion 10-14 mm and TFdi 30-36% are optimal cutoffs for predicting extubation success  
- TFdi 29% under pressure support ventilation predicts increased duration of mechanical ventilation and ICU mortality (85% sensitivity, 88% specificity)  
- Diaphragm thickening fraction has the highest accuracy among respiratory muscle assessments for predicting weaning success  
  
**Predicting NIV Failure:**  

| Clinical Context                  | TFdi Cutoff | Sensitivity | Specificity |
| --------------------------------- | ----------- | ----------- | ----------- |
| De-novo acute respiratory failure | 36-37%      | High        | High        |
| COVID-19 pneumonia on CPAP        | 21.4%       | 94.4%       | 88.9%       |
| AECOPD on NIV (2-hour assessment) | 20%         | 92%         | 93%         |
| AECOPD tidal breathing            | 20%         | AUC 0.84    | —           |
  
**Monitoring Ventilator-Induced Diaphragmatic Dysfunction (VIDD):**
- >10% decrease from baseline thickness indicates clinically significant atrophy  
- Largest decrease occurs during first 72 hours of mechanical ventilation 
- Mean thickness decreases by approximately 32% from baseline

## Diaphragm Dysfunction After Cardiac Interventions
**Mechanisms of Phrenic Nerve Injury in Open Cardiac Surgery:**
1. **Cold-induced injury (most common):** Topical hypothermia with ice slush carries 8-fold higher risk vs. cold saline alone  
2. **Mechanical injury:** Direct transection, stretching during retraction, compression from instruments or chest tubes  
3. **Chest tube positioning:** Tubes looping apicomedially at second right intercostal space carry 4.22-fold increased risk of right diaphragm paralysis
  
**Transcatheter Tricuspid Valve Replacement (TTVR) Considerations:**[[
- TTVR eliminates traditional surgical risks (sternotomy, cardiopulmonary bypass, topical hypothermia)  
- New-onset conduction disturbances occur in 13.5-44% of patients, with 14-28% requiring permanent pacemaker  
- Phrenic nerve injury has not been specifically reported as a complication in published TTVR series  
- Theoretical mechanisms of nerve injury include catheter manipulation, device deployment, and radial force from self-expanding valves near the right phrenic nerve  
  
**Anatomical Vulnerability.
- Right phrenic nerve courses along lateral superior vena cava (minimum distance 0.3 ± 0.5 mm) 
- Proximity to right superior pulmonary vein (2.1 ± 0.4 mm minimum)  
- Vulnerable during catheter-based interventions in the right atrium and superior vena cava  
  
**Prevention Strategies:** 
- Phrenic nerve insulation pads reduce incidence from 18% to 0% in open surgery  
- Intraoperative phrenic nerve monitoring can detect injury in real-time  
- Cold-induced injuries generally have better prognosis than mechanical injuries

## Case Example: Post-TTVR Right Hemidiaphragm Paralysis
**Patient Measurements:**  

| Parameter | Left Hemidiaphragm | Right Hemidiaphragm |  
|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|  
| Inspiratory thickness | 0.66 cm (6.6 mm) | 0.16 cm (1.6 mm) |  
| Expiratory thickness | 0.23 cm (2.3 mm) | 0.13 cm (1.3 mm) |  
| TFdi | 68% | 19% |  
| Excursion | 2.17 cm | None appreciated |  
  
**Interpretation:**  
- **Right hemidiaphragm paralysis confirmed:** TFdi 19% (20% threshold), absent excursion, atrophic thickness (1.3-1.6 mm)  
- **Left hemidiaphragm compensatory hyperactivity:** TFdi 68% (above normal range of 37-65%), excursion 2.17 cm (exceeds typical 1.5-1.8 cm)  
- **Bilateral paralysis excluded:** Robust left-sided function with compensatory hyperactivity  
- **Etiology:** Likely phrenic nerve injury during TTVR procedure, though mechanism differs from traditional open surgery  
  
---  
## Prognosis and Recovery
**Recovery Expectations:
- Cold-induced injuries generally have better prognosis than mechanical injuries  
- Approximately 54-60% of patients with phrenic nerve injury regain diaphragm function  
- Recovery typically occurs over months, with majority of improvement within first year  
- Serial ultrasound monitoring can track recovery by documenting return of thickening and excursion  
  
**Management Considerations:**  
- Respiratory physiotherapy and pulmonary rehabilitation  
- Noninvasive ventilation support as needed  
- Surgical plication may be considered for persistent symptomatic unilateral paralysis  
- Serial diaphragm ultrasound to monitor for recovery  
  
---  
  

## Key Takeaways  
1. **TFdi 20%** during deep inspiration is diagnostic of diaphragm paralysis  
2. **Compensatory hyperactivity** of the contralateral hemidiaphragm distinguishes unilateral from bilateral paralysis  
3. **TFdi 29-36%** predicts NIV failure and prolonged mechanical ventilation  
4. **Serial measurements** are essential for detecting VIDD and monitoring recovery  
5. **Post-cardiac intervention patients** are at risk for phrenic nerve injury through multiple mechanisms  
6. **TTVR** eliminates traditional cold-injury risk but may introduce novel mechanisms of phrenic nerve injury
